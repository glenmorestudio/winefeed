Australian harvest hits 25-year low as demand and weather crush output

  • Australia's 2026 wine harvest fell to 1.27 million tonnes, down 19% from 2025 and 25% below the 10-year average, the lowest in 25 years.
  • Vintage value plummeted 26% to A$837 million despite the smaller crop, with grape prices declining 6% on average year-on-year.
  • Red varieties drove the decline, accounting for 80% of the output loss, while white grapes fell 9% and now comprise the majority of crush for only the second time in 12 years.
  • Peter Bailey, market insights manager at Wine Australia, attributed the sustained underperformance to a structural reset in global demand rather than temporary conditions.
  • Late frosts, heatwaves and flooding across Australian regions compounded weak export demand, with no price recovery anticipated for red wine despite significant vineyard culling.

Rosé's growth risks derailment if producers strip away taste for leanness

  • Rosé is among wine's few growth categories, beloved across age groups from Jeremy Clarkson to Kylie Jenner, while most wine regions face oversupply and falling consumption.
  • The category's appeal rests on balance: dry and crisp yet soft, with red-berry and stone-fruit weight, avoiding the divisiveness of Sauvignon Blanc or delicacy of Pinot Grigio.
  • Patrick Schmitt MW observed in his Global Rosé Masters blind tasting that many producers are trending toward excessive leanness, herbaceous notes, and hardness, abandoning the ripe-fruit texture that built rosé's success.
  • This stylistic drift mirrors Chardonnay's failed "cool-climate" swing toward skinny, buttery excess; consumers rejected that shift, and rosé risks similar backlash if producers over-correct.
  • Schmitt urges everyday rosé to retain moderate alcohol and fruit ripeness while encouraging experimentation at the premium end; drinkers seeking dilution will ice their own glasses.

Texas A&M sends grape seeds to space station to study radiation effects

  • Hundreds of grape seeds from Texas will orbit the International Space Station for six months as part of a Texas A&M University study of cosmic radiation's impact on plant genetics.
  • Aerospace students Coby Arnold and Arvind Subramanyam designed a protective carrier allowing seeds to survive radiation exposure while triggering measurable genetic changes.

Grape sunburn shifts vineyard practices as heat waves reach cooler regions

  • Sunburn in grapes is a stress response to excess light and extreme berry-surface heat; direct sun can raise fruit temperature 22-27°F above air temperature, triggering cell death and flavor damage within minutes.
  • Chris Tanghe, director of education for GuildSomm, identifies sunburned wine by flattened fruit, oxidized bitterness, and notes of rubber, asphalt, and creosote; the damage also produces high alcohol, low acidity, and premature aging.
  • Producers including Tyrrell's in Hunter Valley and Chêne Bleu in Ventoux have stopped or severely restricted summer leaf removal and shifted to kaolin clay sprays, which cool canopies up to 9 degrees Celsius in trials.
  • As climate change pushes heat waves into traditionally moderate regions, vineyard design and canopy management, once tools for ripeness, are becoming tools for protection against fruit damage.

Mourvèdre Thrives in Heat but Struggles With Drought as French Producers Adapt

  • Mourvèdre, long prized in Bandol for heat tolerance, now faces new stress from severe drought and erratic weather, forcing producers to rethink canopy and irrigation practices.
  • Jean-François Ott at Domaines Ott and others are experimenting with higher gobelet training and dense canopy management to shade grapes from extreme sun and slow ripening cycles.
  • A three-year drought in Jumilla from 2022 to 2024 killed even old bush vines, pushing growers to adopt irrigation despite fears it will dilute terroir expression.
  • Bandol's cooler sites traditionally reserved for rosé are now being planted with red varieties as climate shifts, potentially reducing rosé production from its current 75 percent of regional output.
  • Ground cover crops and deeper vine root systems help retain soil moisture, though experts debate whether some high-altitude parcels offer long-term survival odds as heat and dryness intensify.
