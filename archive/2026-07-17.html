US buyers drive fine wine market stability in first half 2026

  • US fine wine buyers' share of purchase value rose to 26.9% in Q2 2026, up from 20.7% in 2025, as participants navigated new tariffs and depleted stocks.
  • Liv-ex data shows US buyers paid an average 1.1% above market price in Q2, compared to 0.03% in Q1, actively supporting price stability.
  • Asian buyers retreated as shipping costs surged up to 60% due to the Iran War, softening local trade prices despite global stabilisation.
  • All major fine wine indices stabilised in H1 2026, though limited trade volume growth indicates buyers remain cautious despite selective opportunity.
  • Private collectors now drive the market as merchants reduce stock purchases and stay risk-averse, making geopolitical shocks a direct threat to valuations.

Australian wine harvest hits 25-year low as demand and weather collapse

  • Australia's 2026 crush totalled 1.27 million tonnes, down 19% year-on-year and 25% below the 10-year average, according to Wine Australia.
  • The vintage value dropped 26% to A$837 million despite the smaller harvest, with grape prices falling 6% on average from 2025.
  • Red varieties bore 80% of the decline; white grapes now comprise the majority of the crush for only the second time in 12 years, signalling a shift in global consumer preference.
  • Late frosts, heatwaves and flooding across Australian regions compounded weak global demand, with Peter Bailey of Wine Australia warning that four consecutive vintages below average suggest a structural reset in grape requirements.
  • The smaller harvest will yield 33 million fewer nine-litre cases, reflecting both shrinking red wine demand globally and Australia's persistent oversupply of reds.

US restaurants' six-times wine markups drive drinkers to drink at home first

  • New York restaurants now mark up wine bottles six times wholesale cost, versus the old four-times convention, pricing a €1.70 Prosecco at $60, according to Gino Colangelo, founder of New York wine PR agency Colangelo & Partners.
  • By-the-glass markups have reached $12-16 per pour, with some Manhattan restaurants' cheapest bottles starting at $100, deterring entry-level drinkers and younger diners unfamiliar with wine lists.
  • On-trade wine sales in the US have fallen roughly 25% since 2019, while ready-to-drink cocktails gained share and fine-dining outlets shrunk, per Southern Glazer's Wine and Spirits and NielsenIQ data.
  • More than 75% of US wine drinkers told the Wine Market Council they will not pay over $16 per glass, yet restaurants continue raising prices to offset rising rent and staffing costs.
  • Consumers are increasingly preloading alcohol at home before dining out to avoid inflated restaurant markups, shifting spending away from on-premise venues and toward retail.

Sicilia DOC unifies regional identity after decade-long negotiations

  • Sicilia DOC was established in 2011 after years of negotiation to create a unified regional brand, competing against narrower sub-regional designations like Etna DOC.
  • The appellation faced slow adoption initially because individual producers preferred their own sub-regional identities, but has now become an important market force.
  • Post-COVID travel and The White Lotus television series filmed in Taormina have boosted Sicily's profile, driving American consumer interest in indigenous grapes like Grillo and Nero d'Avola.
  • Sicilia DOC producers like Tasca d'Almerita range from dessert wines sourced in Pantelleria to minerally whites from Mount Etna slopes, using the broad designation to market diverse styles.
  • The appellation balances simplification for consumers against over-reduction, allowing producers to claim regional identity without obscuring individual terroir and grape variety.

Texas A&M sends grape seeds to space station to study radiation effects

  • Texas A&M University aerospace and viticulture researchers will send hundreds of grape seeds to the International Space Station for a six-month mission studying cosmic radiation's impact on plant genetics.
  • Aerospace engineering students Coby Arnold and Arvind Subramanyam designed a protective carrier allowing seeds to survive space exposure while experiencing measurable genetic changes from radiation.
  • Upon return, seeds will be planted at AgriLife Research vineyard at Thomas Ranch alongside control seeds that remained on Earth to compare growth, genetics, and grape production.
  • Three varieties selected include Lomanto, a Texas cultivar developed by viticulturist T.V. Munson in the early 20th century, chosen for disease resistance and climate tolerance.
  • If successful, researchers led by viticulture specialist Justin Scheiner could eventually produce wine from radiation-exposed seeds, potentially improving future grape breeding and crop resilience.

Grape sunburn spreads north as heat waves force vineyard practice overhaul

  • Sunburn occurs when berry surface temperatures exceed 104-109°F under high light, causing chlorophyll and aromatic compounds to degrade and producing flavors of leather, asphalt, and creosote.
  • Joanna Gambetta, wine scientist at University of Newcastle Australia, found that berries exposed to defoliation at flowering build photoprotective compounds, while defoliation at veraison increases damage risk.
  • Producers in warm regions including Provence and Napa are abandoning or retiming midsummer leaf removal and shifting to reflective kaolin clay sprays and canopy-cover strategies instead.
  • Steve Matthiasson, winemaker at Matthiasson Wines in Napa Valley, emphasizes vineyard design, orientation, trellis architecture, and shoot positioning, to minimize direct fruit exposure.
  • As heat waves arrive earlier and further north, sunburn sorting at harvest has become critical; producers recognize that sunburned fruit contaminates finished wine regardless of vintage quality.

Heat-tolerant Mourvèdre struggles as southern France grows hotter and drier

  • Mourvèdre, the heat-tolerant grape long dominant in Bandol and Jumilla, faces new stress as temperatures rise and droughts intensify across southern France.
  • Producers in Bandol are experimenting with canopy management, higher pruning, and shade-planting to prevent sunburn and slow ripening cycles as heatwaves intensify.
  • A severe three-year drought from 2022 to 2024 in Jumilla killed some old bush vines despite their deep roots, forcing growers to consider irrigation for survival.
  • Jumilla's vineyard area has halved in 20 years as hotter southern sites are abandoned; cooler, high-altitude parcels around 600 to 800 meters remain viable.
  • Bandol's cooler rosé sites are being converted to red production as climate shifts, threatening the region's tradition of 75 percent rosé output.
