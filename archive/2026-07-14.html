Champagne houses mapped by flavor profile and texture

Climate change brings grape sunburn to new regions; growers reverse leaf-removal practices

  • Grape sunburn is a stress response to excess light, UV radiation, and berry-surface heat; direct sun can push berries 22-27°F above air temperature, causing cellular damage and flavor degradation.
  • Chris Tanghe, MS, director of education for GuildSomm, identifies sunburned grapes by flattened fruit, oxidized bitterness, and notes of rubber, asphalt, and creosote; the damage is distinct from simple overripeness.
  • Joanna Gambetta, Ph.D., wine scientist at University of Newcastle, Australia, found that Chardonnay berries show symptoms within five minutes at surface temperatures of 104-109°F under high light.
  • Producers in Provence and Napa, including Chêne Bleu, Tyrrell's, and Matthiasson Wines, have abandoned mid-summer defoliation and now use kaolin clay sprays, earlier leaf removal, or canopy management to protect fruit.
  • Heat waves arriving earlier and further into traditionally cool regions are forcing vineyards worldwide to prioritize shade over sun exposure, fundamentally reversing decades of leaf-removal orthodoxy.

France's heatwave cuts harvests across Champagne, Bordeaux, Burgundy

  • Extreme heat and drought in France are reducing grape yields significantly in major wine regions, with Champagne expecting 10% lower production and Bordeaux potentially facing 20% or greater declines.
  • High temperatures accelerate ripening and raise alcohol levels, risking grape imbalance; some estates are irrigating despite AOC restrictions to save vines as ground temperatures approach 50°C.
  • French regions are harvesting earlier than ever, Champagne around August 15, potentially the earliest on record, adapting to climate change that has steadily shifted the growing cycle earlier over the past decade.
  • Global wine shortages are unlikely due to weak demand and excess inventory, but premium Burgundy whites and Sauternes face tighter supplies as Pinot Noir and entry-level Bordeaux remain oversupplied.
  • Quality remains uncertain until grapes are tasted; small berry size and potential incomplete phenolic maturation could compromise wines despite apparent ripeness if drought persists.

  • Bandol's rosé production, which accounts for 75 percent of output, may decline as cooler sites traditionally reserved for rosé are converted to red wine as growers shift to climate-resilient parcels.

10Italy Matters·Robert Camuto·4 JUL 2026

The Ghosts of California Vino

  • Italian Swiss Colony, founded in 1881 by Genovese immigrant Andrea Sbarbaro near Cloverdale, Sonoma County, was California's most important winery before Napa's rise and the Judgment of Paris.
  • Sbarbaro built the world's largest wine cistern holding 500,000 gallons and planted Italian varietals including Nebbiolo, Grignolino, Barbera, and Sangiovese in the colony's vineyards.
  • Pre-Prohibition, Italian Swiss Colony produced millions of gallons from nine California wineries and exported globally, hiring French Champagne master Charles Jadeau in 1909 to make Golden State Extra Dry Champagne.
  • Prohibition shifted the colony from wine production to selling grapes and dried grape bricks for home use; post-Prohibition, Americans demanded high-alcohol sweet wines over Sbarbaro's dry European-style wines, causing the market to shrink.
  • E & J Gallo purchased Italian Swiss Colony in 2015; today it produces mostly Gallo brands like Orin Swift, Ravenswood, and Souverain in a fraction of its original space.
