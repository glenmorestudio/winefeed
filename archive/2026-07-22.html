Stag's Leap 1973 Cabernet sells for $22,500, five times estimate, at Christie's

  • A bottle of Stag's Leap Wine Cellars 1973 Cabernet Sauvignon, the wine that defeated French classics at the 1976 Judgment of Paris blind tasting, sold for US$22,500 at Christie's online auction against a US$4,000-6,000 estimate.
  • The 1973 vintage, produced by founder Warren Winiarski, became iconic after outscoring France's top reds in the historic tasting organised by Steven Spurrier and Patricia Gallagher with nine French judges.
  • The sale was the centrepiece of a 50-lot Christie's auction marking the 50th anniversary of the Judgment of Paris and featuring rare Stag's Leap vintages from 1974 to 2023.
  • Two other major lots in the auction underperformed: a 1974-2023 vertical and a 55-bottle mixed lot both failed to meet their pre-sale estimates.
  • Juan Muñoz-Oca, estates director at Stag's Leap Wine Cellars, called the record price a testament to the winery's enduring legacy and Napa Valley's standing among the world's great wine regions.

Trump imposes 50% tariff on Canadian alcohol starting August 19

  • President Donald Trump announced a 50 percent tariff on Canadian alcohol effective August 19, citing Canada's ongoing provincial ban on US beverages.
  • Canada's boycott of US alcohol, enacted March 2024 after Trump's first tariffs, cut American wine exports 78 percent and spirits 70 percent in 2025.
  • Only Alberta and Saskatchewan ended the ban in June 2025; other provinces maintain the restriction that stripped US products from shelves.
  • Trump issued the tariff under Section 338 of the Tariff Act of 1930, after the Supreme Court struck down his broader tariffs as unconstitutional earlier this year.
  • Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney and a White House official both signaled willingness to negotiate before the August 19 deadline.

Bordeaux vineyard prices collapse as sales fall below three million hectolitres

  • Bordeaux shipments fell to 2.98 million hectolitres in the 12 months to March 2026, down 12% year-on-year and below three million for the first time in decades.
  • Pauillac vineyard land fell 22.7% to €1.7m per hectare since 2018; Margaux fell 27.3% to €800,000; Saint-Émilion fell 25.9% to €200,000.
  • Lesser appellations suffered steeper falls: Médoc collapsed 81.8% to €10,000 per hectare, Saint-Émilion Satellites fell 73.7% to €25,000, Bordeaux Rouge fell 60.6%.
  • Between 2023 and 2026, almost 30,000 hectares of Bordeaux vines were uprooted; about two-thirds received government compensation of €4,000 or €6,000 per hectare.
  • Producers including Xavier Buffo at Château de la Rivière in Fronsac are removing less profitable vineyards while acquiring land in stronger terroirs.

Spanish sparkling wine producers debate dropping Cava name for premium tiers

  • Carlos López de la Calle, technical director at Bodega Artadi, argued at London's Taste Spain event that Spain's finest sparkling wines must abandon the Cava name, calling it too generic and damaged by mass production.
  • Recaredo and Gramona, premium Penedès producers, left the Cava regulatory body to create Corpinnat in 2015, a stricter collective mark now with 23 members, using native Spanish Xarel-lo and releasing age-worthy bottles after 10 years cellaring.
  • Sarah Jane Evans, Master of Wine and author, proposed splitting Cava: wines aged under 18 months retain the name, while older, individual expressions from Catalonia, Rioja, or Requena should be renamed by region to signal prestige.
  • López de la Calle suggested Penedès as the most obvious regional name, with potential for Galicia and northern Chacolí, each offering unique conditions for sparkling production unavailable under a generic Cava label.
  • Cava DO president Javier Pagés countered that the brand's global recognition and focus on quality and sustainability, including 100% organic Cava de Guarda Superior from 2025, are key to its success.

Young fine-wine collectors worldwide follow identical paths, Areni Global study finds

  • Areni Global surveyed 308 respondents aged under 40 across London, Paris, New York, Shanghai, Hong Kong and Singapore in February 2026, defining fine wine as bottles at £50 or above.
  • Young collectors in all six cities learn about wine through friends and social networks rather than family, transitioning from casual buyers to collectors between ages 26 and 40.
  • East Asian collectors pursue wine education as their entry point, while Western collectors typically encounter wine through workplace or social connections with established enthusiasts.
  • More women are purchasing expensive wines in Asia than the West, attributed to financial independence and wine's later arrival as a gender-neutral collectible rather than male-exclusive status symbol.
  • Wine educator Corinne Mui in Hong Kong disputed the study's price threshold, arguing fine wine requires ageing potential and that most collectors prize exclusivity and club membership status over transparency.

Château Galoupet redesigns Provence rosé through regenerative farming and climate adaptation

  • Mathieu Meyer, technical director at LVMH-backed Château Galoupet in La Londe since 2019, shifted the 65-hectare estate to regenerative organic farming certified in 2025.
  • Cover crops ploughed into soil and contour trenches capture rainfall to build organic matter and manage drought, increasing insect predators and reducing pest control need.

Grape sunburn damage spreads north as heat waves intensify, forcing vineyard practice overhaul

  • Sunburn in grapes is a stress response to excess light and heat that degrades chlorophyll, polyphenols, carotenoids, and aromatic compounds, producing flavors of cooked fruit, asphalt, rubber, and creosote in finished wine.
  • Berry surface temperatures can reach 122°F on a 95°F day; Chardonnay shows damage within five minutes at 104-109°F under high light, while cell death occurs faster at 126°F.
  • Defoliation timing now determines sunburn risk: early defoliation at flowering allows berries to build photoprotective compounds, while midsummer or veraison defoliation before heat waves dramatically increases damage.

Heat-Tolerant Mourvèdre Struggles as Southern France Gets Hotter

  • Mourvèdre, a late-ripening grape that thrives in heat, is the main red variety in France's Bandol region and Spain's Jumilla, where it needs long, hot growing seasons to ripen fully.
  • Even Mourvèdre is losing balance and elegance as heatwaves intensify; France recorded its highest average temperature on 23 June, with harvests earlier and acidity lower each year.
  • Bandol producers including Jean-François Ott at Château Romassan and the Gueissard family are using canopy shade techniques, higher pruning, and ground cover to protect grapes from sunburn and retain soil moisture.
  • A three-year drought in Jumilla from 2022 to 2024 killed old Monastrell vines; Carolina Martínez Origone, general secretary of the Jumilla Consejo, warns irrigation may now be essential for vine survival.
  • Bandol's cooler sites once reserved for rosé are now being used for red wine as the region adapts, risking a shift away from rosé production, currently 75 percent of regional output.

  • Octavian Wine data shows 78% of white wines shipped to collectors are from the previous six vintages, versus 40% of reds, revealing white wines are consumed far younger.
