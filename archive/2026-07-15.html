Champagne houses map their flavor profiles by grape blend and aging time

  • Champagne's three permitted grapes, Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, and Pinot Meunier, create distinct flavor profiles: Chardonnay-dominant blends taste mineral and light; Pinot Noir-heavy blends taste richer and fruit-forward.
  • Extended aging on dead yeast cells (lees) during Champagne's second fermentation develops brioche and almond notes; longer aging produces bready, buttery flavors and creamy texture, increasing body weight.
  • Winemakers distinguish house style through two key choices: grape blend ratios and lees-aging duration, with minimum required aging of twelve months but optional extension at disgorgement.
  • Champagne houses position themselves along two axes: mineral-to-creamy flavor and light-to-full body, allowing consumers to identify which house style matches their taste preference.

French wine regions adopt back-sweetening to court younger drinkers

  • Back-sweetening, adding sweetness to finished table wine before bottling, is reshaping global wine styles as producers target casual drinkers seeking softer, fruitier wines with lower alcohol.
  • France's INAO approved back-sweetening up to 9 grams per litre in November 2025; Bordeaux and Rhône regions are now trialling the process to broaden their product ranges and respond to climate change.

  • The shift marks the most significant change in wine styles since the 1980s, when critic Robert Parker and winemaker Michel Rolland popularised big, fruity, high-alcohol reds.

Grape sunburn becomes urgent problem as heat waves spread to cooler regions

  • Sunburn in grapes is a stress response to excess light and extreme berry-surface heat, direct sun can raise fruit temperature 22-27°F above air temperature, sometimes exceeding 122°F on exposed sides.
  • Chris Tanghe, MS, director of education for GuildSomm, describes sunburned wine as having flattened fruit, oxidized bitterness, and notes of rubber, asphalt, and creosote; symptoms worsen as wine ages.
  • Joanna Gambetta, Ph.D., wine scientist at University of Newcastle Australia, distinguishes sunburn browning (cosmetic yellowing) from sunburn necrosis (cell death); Chardonnay berries show damage within five minutes at 104-109°F under high light.
  • Producers are abandoning traditional midsummer defoliation and east-side leaf removal, instead using canopy cover, kaolin clay sprays (cooling vines up to 9°C at Tyrrell's in Hunter Valley), and earlier defoliation timing.
  • Climate change is pushing heat waves into traditionally cool regions, forcing winemakers to prioritize shade and sorting as defenses; practices once safe in moderate summers now risk crop damage.

  • Bandol's wine style is shifting as cooler rosé parcels are converted to red production, raising questions about whether the region's 75 percent rosé output will decline.

Osaka researchers develop high-ornithine yeast strain for craft beer

  • Scientists at Osaka-based institutions led by Hiroshi Takagi developed ADHorn49, a yeast strain that produces nine times more ornithine than its parent strain using conventional breeding, not genetic modification.
  • Ornithine is an amino acid marketed in health supplements for fatigue reduction and sleep improvement; the new yeast allows brewers to boost ornithine content without genetic engineering.
