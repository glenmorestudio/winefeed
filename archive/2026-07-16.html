Rioja brands including Federico Paternina go to auction in July insolvency sale

  • IAG Auction will sell four major Rioja trademarks from Marqués de la Concordia Group on 27-29 July as part of insolvency proceedings involving United Wineries entities.
  • The portfolio includes Federico Paternina, Lagunilla, Marqués de la Concordia and Rioja Santiago, all with decades or over a century of family ownership and international recognition.
  • Between 2019-21 the four brands generated €18.49 million in normalised Rioja DO sales, with Federico Paternina earning 52% and Lagunilla 29% of that total.
  • Exports represented 41% of historical sales, with Nordic countries accounting for 27.72% of overseas revenue and Central Europe for 21.93%, offering geographic diversification.
  • The sale is open to wine producers, industry operators, investment funds and international companies seeking to acquire established brands rather than build new ones from scratch.

Kopke, Tradición, Argyros win DWWA 2026 Best in Show for aged fortified wines

  • Decanter World Wine Awards 2026 named four Best in Show winners, all sweet or fortified wines: Kopke 1957 Colheita Port, Bodegas Tradición VORS 30 Years Amontillado Sherry, Estate Argyros Vinsanto Late Release 2005, and Oremus Aszú 6 Puttonyos 2016 Tokaji.

  • Oremus's Tokaji involved manual selection of botrytised berries mixed at a 6 puttonyos ratio into fermenting juice, then aged two years in Hungarian oak barrels plus 4-6 years post-bottling.

French regulators approve back-sweetening of table wines up to 9g/l

  • France's INAO approved back-sweetening of table wines up to 9 grams per litre in November 2025, allowing producers to add sweetness after fermentation but before bottling.
  • Bordeaux and Rhône regions are now trialling sweeter wines to match consumer demand for softer, fruitier, lower-alcohol styles, with Côtes du Rhône seeking approval for up to 9g/l sugar.
  • Sweeter wines by the glass are already common in US casual dining and spreading in France, marketed as entry points for younger, less experienced drinkers into wine.
  • Back-sweetened table wines contain far more residual sugar than traditional dry wines, which typically hold under 5g/l; Apothic red contained 16.4g/l when it reached UK shelves in 2013.

Grape sunburn spreads north as heat waves arrive earlier and hotter

  • Sunburn in grapes is a stress response to excess light and extreme berry surface heat, not simple overripeness. Direct sun can push berry temperature 22-27°F above air temperature, causing chlorophyll and polyphenol breakdown.
  • Chris Tanghe, MS, director of education for GuildSomm, identified sunburned fruit by sensory markers: flattened fruit, oxidized bitterness, and notes of asphalt, rubber, and creosote that intensify as wine ages.
  • Joanna Gambetta, Ph.D., wine scientist at University of Newcastle, found Chardonnay berries show damage within five minutes at surface temperatures of 104-109°F under high light, and necrosis kills cells at 126°F.
  • Traditional mid-summer defoliation now increases sunburn risk in warming regions. Producers including Chêne Bleu in Ventoux and Tyrrell's in Hunter Valley have shifted to late-flowering defoliation, kaolin clay sprays, and canopy retention to protect fruit.
  • Steve Matthiasson, winemaker at Matthiasson Wines in Napa, emphasizes upstream vineyard design, trellis architecture and shoot placement, over sun exposure to preserve color, acidity, and aromatic compounds.

Mourvèdre thrives in heat but struggles as climate swings to extremes

  • Mourvèdre, long grown in hot Bandol and Spanish Jumilla for its heat tolerance, now faces stress from extreme temperatures and multi-year droughts that even old vines cannot survive.
  • Bandol producers including Jean-François Ott at Château Romassan and the Gueissards are experimenting with canopy management and cover crops to shade grapes and retain soil moisture.
  • Jumilla's planted area halved in 20 years as southern, hotter sites were abandoned; growers now concentrate on higher-altitude parcels with deeper soils and stronger water retention.
  • Irrigation, once rejected by dry-farming purists in Bandol and Jumilla, is becoming essential during severe droughts; Bandol director Olivier Colombano warns vines will die without it in extreme years.
  • Climate shifts are reshaping wine styles: cooler Bandol sites traditionally used for rosé are being converted to red wine, potentially reducing the region's 75 percent rosé output.

Marketing Natural Wine

  • Natural wine is the leading opportunity in wine categories according to IWSR's Opportunity Index and expands globally at around 13% annually, far outpacing the struggling broader wine market.
  • Georgia defines natural wine as wine from spontaneous fermentation of organically grown grapes with minimal oenological practices, limiting sulphur dioxide to a maximum of 40 mg/l.
  • Research by Sophie Parker-Thomson MW found wines with no added SO₂ contained the highest levels of biogenic amines, contradicting the natural wine movement's focus on sulphur dioxide as a problem.
  • Natural wine producers use bolder, more intentional design and branding, with creative names, artistic labels, and quirky closures, but don't necessarily spend more money than conventional producers.
  • Natural wine has successfully captured Gen Z consumers and achieved mainstream acceptance, with natural wine bars proliferating across major cities including 600 in Paris alone.
