Champagne caps 2026 production at 250m bottles, lowest since Covid.

  • Comité Champagne set the 2026 marketable yield at 8,800kg/ha, equivalent to 250 million bottles, the lowest since 2020's pandemic cap of 8,000kg/ha.
  • The limit marks a fourth consecutive year of cuts: down from 9,000kg/ha in 2025, 10,000kg/ha in 2024, 11,400kg/ha in 2023, and 12,000kg/ha in 2022.
  • Growers and winemakers imposed the ceiling to prevent overproduction beyond market demand and to gradually reduce excess inventory while maintaining vineyard profitability.
  • First-half 2025 shipments grew 1.2% to 107.1 million bottles; if sustained, annual shipments would reach 269 million, above last year's 266 million.
  • The 2026 growing season was marked by frost, June heat, and drought, with harvest expected 10 to 15 days earlier than normal, starting around 20 August.

Moldova stakes claim as serious wine nation with native grapes and vast cellars

  • Moldova has 114,000 hectares of vineyards covering 7% of its land, the highest vineyard density globally, with wine accounting for 3% of GDP and over 8% of exports.
  • The country sits on chernozem, the black soil covering only 1.8% of Earth's surface, enabling yields of 8-12 tonnes per hectare without irrigation.
  • Cricova and Mileștii Mici cellars are former limestone quarries with 70km and 200km of driveable tunnels respectively, providing ideal natural conditions for traditional method sparkling wines.
  • Fetească family grapes, Albă, Regála, and Neagră, are indigenous varieties with no global equivalents, offering floral whites and dark-fruited reds comparable to Pinot and Malbec but with Eastern European character.
  • Rară Neagră and Viorica are distinctive varieties grown primarily in Moldova; Divin brandy holds protected geographical indication status equivalent to Cognac since 2012.

  • Costco sold desert willow plants carrying glassy-winged sharpshooter eggs between 24 June and 3 July 2026, triggering a California recall.

Grape sunburn spreads beyond hot climates as heat waves shift vineyard practices

  • Sunburn occurs when berry surface temperatures exceed 104-109°F under high light, causing chlorophyll and polyphenols to break down within minutes, producing cooked fruit, asphalt, and creosote aromas.
  • Chris Tanghe, director of education at GuildSomm, identified sunburned grapes by their flattened fruit character, oxidized bitterness, and rubber and asphalt notes during a 2025 TexSom seminar.
  • Joanna Gambetta, wine scientist at the University of Newcastle, Australia, found that early defoliation at flowering reduces sunburn more than late defoliation at veraison because berries build photoprotective compounds.
  • Producers from Provence to Napa are abandoning midsummer leaf removal and adopting kaolin clay sprays and canopy-focused vineyard design, with Tyrrell's in Hunter Valley reporting 9°C canopy cooling from kaolin treatment.
  • Heat waves arriving earlier and further into traditionally cool regions are making sunburn prevention critical; undetected sunburned grapes produce premature aging, flabby acid, and brown-rimmed color in finished wine.

Heat-Tolerant Mourvèdre Struggles as Southern France Temperatures Soar

  • Mourvèdre, long valued for heat tolerance in Bandol and Spain's Jumilla region, now faces stress from record temperatures and prolonged drought that even this variety cannot always survive.
  • A three-year drought from 2022 to 2024 in Jumilla killed some old Mourvèdre bush vines despite their deep roots, forcing growers to irrigate or abandon parcels in the hottest southern areas.
  • Bandol producers are adapting canopy management, raising gobelet training height, and planting shade trees to protect grapes from sunburn and slow ripening cycles as summers intensify.
  • Irrigation, traditionally rejected in dry-farmed Bandol to preserve terroir character, is now essential in some parcels; without it, vines face death before harvest.
  • Wine style is shifting as producers move production from traditionally cooler rosé sites to red wine parcels, and Bandol's rosé output at 75 percent of total production may decline.
