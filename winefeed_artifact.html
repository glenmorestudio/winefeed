Paul Boutinot steps back from wine distributor after five decades

  • Paul Boutinot, founder of UK wine distributor Boutinot Wines, sold the company to its management team in 2013, led by Dennis Whiteley, Michael Moriarty and Tony Brown MW.
  • Boutinot launched his Stockport-based importer in 1980 after working at J.B. Reynier and Augustus Barnett, building it into one of Britain's leading wine traders over three decades.
  • The company pioneered unadorned table wines in the 1980s, with Cuvée Jean-Paul reaching one million bottles annually within eight years despite industry predictions of only three percent market uptake.
  • Boutinot applied his natural fermentation philosophy across France, Australia, Chile and South Africa, controlling production directly rather than merely trading.
  • Since the 2013 handover, Boutinot's focus has shifted to South Africa's Waterkloof estate, where over 20 years of work are advancing his long-term ambitions.

Rosé's growth at risk if producers sacrifice flavour for extreme leanness

  • Rosé is one of few wine categories growing amid oversupply and reduced consumption, appealing across age groups and backed by celebrities from Jeremy Clarkson to Kylie Jenner.
  • The category's success rests on balance: dry and mouthwatering yet soft, with red berry and stone fruit flavours, crisp finish, and structure suited to light meals or aperitifs.
  • Patrick Schmitt MW, tasting judge, detected a stylistic drift towards leanness in many rosés entered in The Global Rosé Masters competition this year, some herbaceous or hard.
  • Schmitt warns the shift mirrors Chardonnay's over-correction away from oak towards thinness, which consumers rejected; rosé's benchmark brands succeeded by staying balanced, not thin.
  • He urges producers to preserve ripe fruit, moderate alcohol, and texture in everyday rosé, reserving experimentation for premium offerings, lest drinkers abandon the category for diluted versions with added ice.

Texas A&M sends grape seeds to space station to study radiation effects

  • Hundreds of grape seeds from Texas will spend six months aboard the International Space Station to measure how cosmic radiation affects plant genetics, led by Texas A&M University researchers.
  • Aerospace engineering students Coby Arnold and Arvind Subramanyam designed a protective carrier that shields seeds from lethal radiation while allowing controlled genetic exposure.

Grape sunburn damages flavor as heat waves spread to cooler regions

  • Sunburn in grapes is a stress response to excess light and heat; berry surface temperatures 22-27°F above air temperature can trigger damage within minutes at 104-109°F.
  • Two forms exist: sunburn browning causes yellow or brown spots on skin; sunburn necrosis kills cells, cracks skins, and shrivels berries, driven primarily by extreme temperature.
  • Damaged grapes produce sensory signatures of flattened fruit, oxidized bitterness, rubber, asphalt, and creosote notes in finished wine; damage compounds as wine ages.
  • Mid-summer defoliation once standard practice now risks sunburn if heat waves arrive shortly after; early defoliation at flowering allows berries to build photoprotective compounds.
  • Producers in Provence and Napa are shifting away from leaf removal, adopting kaolin clay particle films to reflect heat, and redesigning canopy architecture to shade fruit from direct sun.

Heat-Tolerant Mourvèdre Struggles as Southern France Grows Too Hot

  • Mourvèdre, bred for heat and drought, is losing balance and elegance in southern France as temperatures rise and drought intensifies, pushing growers to adapt canopy and soil management.
  • Jean-François Ott, fourth-generation winemaker at Domaines Ott in Bandol, is experimenting with three-dimensional gobelet and échalas vine training to shade grapes from sunburn.
  • A three-year drought from 2022 to 2024 in Jumilla killed even old, deep-rooted bush vines, forcing growers toward irrigation despite concerns over terroir typicity.
  • Bandol's cooler, fresher parcels, traditionally reserved for rosé, are now being converted to red wine production as the region shifts to heat-tolerant sites, potentially reducing rosé output.
  • Jumilla's cultivated surface area halved over 20 years as southern, hotter parcels were abandoned; remaining vineyards cluster at 600-800 metre altitudes with stronger diurnal temperature swings.
